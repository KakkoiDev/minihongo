# Pattern-Based Language Teaching Research

Research into minimal sentence patterns that generate most of a language's expressive power.
Applied to minihongo's goal of making Japanese accessible with minimum complexity.

---

## Origin: 3 Patterns to Build Any Chinese Sentence

**Source:** [Sheldon - 3 Patterns to Build Any Chinese Sentence](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yvBZTBaX0Is)

Sheldon's team reverse-engineered 2,000+ Chinese sentence structures and found that three core patterns generate roughly 80% of everything you need to say. The key insight: stop memorizing, start building.

### Pattern 1: STPVO Blueprint (Set the Stage, Then Act)

Chinese has a fixed word order: Subject - Time - Place - Verb - Object.

- Time and place always come before the verb
- "Lights, camera, action" - establish the scene, then roll
- Like a game of mad libs: five slots, fill them in order, the logic never breaks
- One frame generates thousands of possibilities

Example: I yesterday at-home read book (wo zuotian zai jia kan shu)

This kills the "translation trap" - when you think in English and try to mirror its flexible word order in Chinese, you jam up. Knowing the framework means you just slot things in.

### Pattern 2: Action Chain (Stack Verbs in Order)

English connects actions with "ghost words" (to, by, with, in order to). Chinese removes them entirely - just list actions in the order they happen in real life.

- "I went home to sleep" = I go-home sleep (two actions stacked)
- "I went to China by plane" = I sit-plane go-China (chronological order)
- "I eat with chopsticks" = I use-chopsticks eat-food (tool first, then action)
- You can chain 3, 4, 5+ actions: "I flew to China to see my friend perform a dance" = I sit-plane go-China see-friend perform-dance

Zero prepositions. You're narrating reality exactly as it unfolds. Chinese is literally a timeline.

### Pattern 3: Time Filters (Particles, Not Conjugation)

Chinese doesn't change the verb. You add tiny particles that work like "Instagram filters" on a base sentence:

- **Progressive (right now):** Add 在 (zai) before the verb (Mandarin) or 緊 (gan) after (Cantonese)
- **Completed (past):** Add 了 (le) after the verb or at sentence end
- **Future:** Add 会 (hui) before the verb - same position as English "will"

### Pattern 4: Logic Bridges (Because...So / But)

Chinese requires both parts of cause-effect: because X, so Y. Unlike English which drops the "so." Works like an if-then statement in code.

- Because I was tired, so I went home to sleep
- "But" (但是/可是) works the same as English

## Mapping to Japanese

### Pattern 1: STPVO -> TTPOV (Verb Last)

Japanese maps almost directly to the Chinese pattern, with verb at the end instead of before the object:

| Slot | Chinese | Japanese |
|------|---------|----------|
| 1 | Subject | Topic/Subject (wa/ga) |
| 2 | Time | Time (ni) |
| 3 | Place | Place (de/ni) |
| 4 | Verb | Object (wo) |
| 5 | Object | **Verb (always last)** |

Minihongo's gram-1 currently teaches "verb last" mechanically. The "set the stage" metaphor is more intuitive: [who] [when] [where] [what] -> [action].

### Pattern 2: Action Chain = Te-form

This is the strongest parallel. Te-form chaining is exactly Chinese verb stacking:

- 書いて読んで寝た = wrote, read, slept (three actions in order)
- 家に帰って寝た = went home, slept ("I went home to sleep")
- 箸を使って食べる = use chopsticks, eat ("I eat with chopsticks")

No "ghost words" needed in Japanese either. Te-form chains actions in chronological order, just like Chinese stacks bare verbs.

Current minihongo gram-15 presents te-form as a technical Swiss Army knife with 5+ uses crammed together. The video's framing of "narrate reality in order" is more approachable as an introduction.

### Pattern 3: Filters vs Conjugation

Japanese does conjugate, unlike Chinese. But minihongo already simplifies to a filter-like system:

| Filter | Japanese | Chinese equivalent |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| Base (present/future) | る | base verb |
| Past/completed | た | 了 (le) |
| Negative | ない | 不 (bu) |
| Progressive | ている | 在 (zai) |
| Polite | ます | - (no equivalent) |
| Future | (context/つもり) | 会 (hui) |

The "Instagram filter" metaphor works: raw photo (base verb) + filter (た/ている/ない/ます) = changed meaning without touching any other word.

### Pattern 4: Logic Bridges

Maps directly:

- から...（だから）= because...so (same structure as Chinese)
- でも/けど = but (same as Chinese 但是)
- たら/なら/ければ = if...then (same as Chinese 如果...就)

### What Transfers Well

| Concept | Transfers? | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Stage-then-action word order | Yes | Time/place before verb in both |
| Action chaining (chronological) | Yes | Te-form = Chinese verb stacking |
| No "ghost words" (to/by/with) | Partially | Particles (de/ni) replace some, but aren't prepositions |
| No verb conjugation | No | Japanese conjugates, but minihongo minimizes to 4 forms |
| "Filter" metaphor for tense | Yes | ta/teiru/nai/masu work like filters on a base verb |
| Cause-effect (because...so) | Yes | kara...(dakara) works the same |
| "But" connector | Yes | demo/kedo work similarly |

### Concrete Improvements for Minihongo

1. **Reframe gram-1** with the "set the stage" metaphor instead of dry "verb last" rule. Show it as slot-filling: [who] [when] [where] [what] [action].
2. **Split gram-15** (te-form) - introduce action chaining first as its own concept ("narrate reality in order"), then layer on the other te-form uses later. Currently it's an overwhelming dump of 5 uses at once.
3. **Add a "3 patterns" overview** at the start of grammar - give learners the big picture before details:
   - Pattern 1: Set the stage (word order)
   - Pattern 2: Chain actions (te-form)
   - Pattern 3: Apply filters (tense/politeness)

## Related Research: Pattern-Based Language Teaching

### 80/20 Japanese - Richard Webb

**Source:** [8020japanese.com](https://8020japanese.com/)

The closest match to the Chinese video's approach, applied to Japanese. Webb applies the Pareto Principle: learn the 20% of structures that cover 80% of sentences.

Key contributions:
- **TTPOV framework** (Topic, Time, Place, Object, Verb) - nearly identical to Sheldon's STPVO but with verb at the end
- [Visual guide to Japanese word order](https://8020japanese.com/japanese-word-order/) - slot-based approach
- [Sentence structure guide](https://8020japanese.com/japanese-sentence-structure/) - building sentences from patterns
- Argues against memorizing sentence patterns in favor of understanding how particles organize information around the verb
- 6+ years in Japan, JLPT N1, degree in Japanese, studied linguistics at Japanese university

### Cure Dolly - Organic Japanese

**Source:** [learnjapaneseonline.info](https://learnjapaneseonline.info/) | [Transcripts](https://kellenok.github.io/cure-script/1-the-basic-types-of-sentences.html)

Reduces ALL Japanese sentences to 2-3 core patterns:

1. **A does B** - Sakura ga aruku (Sakura walks)
2. **A is B** - Sakura ga nihonjin da (Sakura is Japanese)
3. **A is [adjective]** - Sakura ga utsukushii (Sakura is beautiful)

Key contributions:
- "Train" metaphor: main carriage (A) + engine (B) = every sentence
- ga is the center of every Japanese sentence - the linchpin that links A and B
- Everything else (particles, conjugation, modifiers) is decoration on these cores
- Same philosophy as Sheldon's "mad libs" - understand the frame, not the exceptions
- Teaches Japanese as Japanese works, not as English mapped into Japanese

### Tim Ferriss - Deconstruction Dozen

**Source:** [How to Learn Any Language in 1 Hour](https://tim.blog/2007/11/07/how-to-learn-but-not-master-any-language-in-1-hour-plus-a-favor/)

Uses 12 pre-selected sentences to decode any language's grammar framework.

Key contributions:
- **DiSSS framework:** Deconstruction, Selection, Sequencing, Stakes
- "What are the minimal learnable units?" - same question as minihongo
- 20% of words make up 80% of everyday conversation (Pareto applied to vocabulary)
- Compression: can you compress the most important 20% into a cheat sheet?
- Practical validation: enough to hold basic conversations in ~2 weeks

### Jay Rubin - Making Sense of Japanese

**Source:** [Making Sense of Japanese: What the Textbooks Don't Tell You](https://www.amazon.com/Making-Sense-Japanese-What-Textbooks/dp/156836492X)

Harvard professor, 30 years of teaching. Debunks textbook myths about Japanese grammar.

Key contributions:
- Japanese is NOT vague - it follows strict logical rules that textbooks oversimplify
- The "subjectless sentence" myth - every sentence has a subject, even when hidden
- Real difference between wa and ga (not the oversimplified textbook version)
- Argues for understanding the system rather than memorizing exceptions
- Helps learners "unlearn" the simplified shortcuts from Genki-style textbooks

### Tae Kim - Guide to Japanese Grammar

**Source:** [guidetojapanese.org](https://www.guidetojapanese.org/)

Already in minihongo's references. Anti-translation approach to Japanese grammar.

Key contributions:
- Teaches Japanese as Japanese works, not as "how to express English in Japanese"
- Uses deliberately literal (grammatically incorrect English) translations to show actual structure
- Same anti-translation-trap philosophy as Sheldon's video
- 50 sections covering all essential grammar from first principles
- Free, widely used, community standard

### Benny Lewis - Fluent in 3 Months

**Source:** [fluentin3months.com](https://www.fluentin3months.com/)

"Speak from day one" philosophy. Less about patterns, more about overcoming the fear barrier.

Key contributions:
- Universal phrases and language hacks over grammar study
- Emphasis on output (speaking) over input (studying)
- Pattern recognition through immersion rather than explicit teaching
- Complements the pattern approach - once you have the patterns, use them immediately

## Common Thread Across All Approaches

All researchers converge on the same thesis:

1. **Stop memorizing, start building** - understand a few generative patterns rather than drilling thousands of sentences
2. **The translation trap** - don't mirror English structure in the target language; learn how the target language actually organizes information
3. **Verb-centric structure** - everything revolves around the verb (last in Japanese, after time/place in Chinese)
4. **Pareto principle** - a handful of patterns generates most sentences
5. **Slot-filling over rules** - think of sentences as templates with slots, not as rules with exceptions
